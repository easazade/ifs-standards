# IFS Standards

_The real solution to freedom._


IFS is a new kind of governance system built around one simple idea: you stay free, even when you work with others. In IFS, you can delegate power without giving it away. You can let others make decisions, but you still own that authority.

Delegation is flexible and controlled. You can limit it, adjust it, or take it back whenever you want. You can also see how your delegated power is being used, so nothing happens in the dark. The system is designed to keep your freedom intact, not slowly take it away over time.

Before talking exactly about what IFS is, we need to talk about what IFS is not.

## What IFS Is Not

To really understand IFS, we first need to look at what it is not.

- It is not a monarchy, because in a monarchy power is tied to a royal family and passed down by inheritance. In IFS, power is not owned by a family or bloodline, it stays with individuals.

- It is not authoritarianism, because in authoritarian systems power is concentrated in one leader or a small group, and people have little real control. In IFS, power remains distributed and individuals stay in control of what they delegate.

- It is not communism, because in communist systems the state typically controls major resources and decision-making is centralized. In IFS, control is not absorbed by a central authority, it stays with people even when they coordinate or delegate power.

- It is not an oligarchy, because in an oligarchy power is held by a small elite group. In IFS, power does not accumulate at the top, it remains with each individual and can be delegated without being lost.

- It is not a technocracy, because in technocracies experts make decisions on behalf of everyone else. In IFS, you can choose to delegate to experts, but you still own the decision power and can take it back.

- It is not a theocracy, because in a theocracy authority is based on religion and controlled by religious leaders. In IFS, authority is not tied to belief systems, it is owned and controlled by individuals.

- It is not a republic in the traditional sense, because even in republics people often hand over power to representatives and lose direct control over it. In IFS, delegation does not mean losing control, it remains reversible and visible at all times.

In short, most existing systems require you to give up power in order to function. IFS is built on the opposite idea: you can share power without ever losing it, and you can always see how it is being used.

## It Is Not Democracy Either

Democracy was a big step forward in its time. It gave people a voice and a way to choose their leaders. But it still has a core problem: you give your power away for a fixed period of time.

When you elect someone, you hand them authority and control over your life for years. During that time, your control is very limited. If you realize a few months later that they are not capable, not aligned with your interests, or simply not doing a good job, there is usually no direct, fast way to correct that. You are mostly locked in until the next election cycle.

There is also a lack of real, practical transparency. In theory, democracies can be transparent. In practice, it is hard for an everyday person to clearly see how decisions are made, why they are made, and whether they truly serve the public or other interests. You cannot easily track every decision or understand how your delegated power is being used.

When power is delegated without continuous visibility and control, it tends to drift and concentrate. Influence accumulates around networks of insiders, funders, and gatekeepers, and over time this can resemble an informal oligarchy even inside a democratic system.

There is also a pattern of weak accountability. Decisions can be justified after the fact with incomplete information, shifting explanations, or claims of good intent. Responsibility becomes diffused across committees, advisors, and processes, making it difficult to pinpoint who is accountable. This creates a kind of plausible deniability, where poor outcomes rarely lead to clear consequences.

Because of this, delegation in democracy is static and slow. Power is assigned in large chunks of time, and changing that assignment is difficult. This made sense in a world without modern technology, but today it feels outdated.

IFS takes a different approach. Instead of giving power away for years, you can delegate it dynamically. You can assign it, adjust it, or take it back at any time. You can also see how it is being used, so delegation does not turn into loss of control.

In IFS, you are not choosing someone to control things on your behalf for a long period. You are continuously in control, while still being able to coordinate and delegate when needed.

## Why IFS Can Exist Now

IFS is not just an idea. It is something that can exist today because the world now has the infrastructure to support it.

First, we now have global connectivity. A large portion of the world’s population has access to the internet, and in many countries the majority of people are online. While access is not universal, it is widespread enough to support systems that rely on continuous communication and participation.

Second, personal computing is everywhere. In the past, very few people had access to computers. Today, billions of people carry smartphones that are more powerful than older computers. This means people can interact with systems like IFS at any time, from anywhere.

Third, we have real-time systems. Modern software, cloud infrastructure, and distributed systems allow information to be updated, tracked, and verified instantly. This makes dynamic delegation, monitoring, and control possible in a way that was not feasible before.

Fourth, we have transparency tools. Technologies like digital ledgers, audit trails, and secure identity systems make it possible to track decisions and actions in a clear and verifiable way. This directly supports the idea of visible and accountable power.

In the past, governance systems had to be static because communication was slow, information was limited, and coordination at scale was difficult. Today, those limitations no longer exist.

IFS becomes possible because the technical foundation now exists to support a system where power can be delegated, monitored, and adjusted in real time.

## IFS Is a Dynamic System

**It is what we want, when we want, and how we want it.**

In IFS, power is not fixed or locked for long periods. It is fluid. You can delegate it, limit it, adjust it, or take it back at any time. That means the system can continuously adapt to what people actually need, instead of forcing everyone to live with decisions made months or years ago.

If something does not work, it does not stay that way. It can be corrected quickly. If a decision is not aligned with what people want, it can be changed. If a better solution appears, it can be adopted without waiting for long cycles or major disruptions.

This answers a common concern: “What if the system becomes something we don’t want?” In IFS, that state cannot persist for long, because people always retain control over delegated power. The system reflects current intent, not outdated decisions.

Instead of static rules and slow correction, IFS is built on continuous adjustment. Your life is not shaped by distant decisions you cannot influence. It is shaped by what you and others actively choose, in real time.

## IFS Is More Than Voting

IFS is not just an online voting system, and it is not simply about electing someone and taking your vote back later.

In IFS, you can directly influence the things that affect your daily life. This includes decisions about local services, how resources are used, and how work is done. If something is not working, you are not limited to waiting or complaining. You can take action, propose changes, and influence outcomes.

At the same time, this does not mean you have to make every decision yourself. Most decisions can still be handled by people you choose and trust, including experts and responsible individuals you delegate to. You can assign responsibility to individuals or groups, define what they are responsible for, and monitor how they perform. IFS gives you the option to stay hands-on where it matters to you, like decisions affecting your daily life or local services, while allowing you to rely on others for broader or more complex responsibilities. This keeps the system practical, without overwhelming individuals, while making delegation a choice, not a loss of control.

IFS also allows new problems to be recognized and new roles to be created. If something needs attention, people can define it, assign responsibility, and shape how it is handled. The system is not limited to predefined structures.

This means IFS combines decision-making, delegation, monitoring, and continuous improvement. It is a complete system for how power is used, not just a mechanism for choosing who holds it.

## IFS Continuously Evolves

IFS is not something that is designed once and then frozen. It improves over time.

Because decisions are made by a large number of people, the system benefits from many perspectives, experiences, and ideas. When better ways of doing things are discovered, they can be adopted. When mistakes are made, they can be corrected. When conditions change, the system can evolve with them.

This creates a feedback loop. People observe outcomes, learn from them, and adjust the system. Over time, this leads to faster improvement and more refined solutions than systems where change is slow and limited.

In traditional systems, evolution is rare and often delayed. In IFS, evolution is continuous. The system gets better because people can continuously shape it.

The result is a governance system that does not become outdated. It adapts, improves, and grows along with the people using it.

## Shared Impact, Shared Voice

Your freedom ends where it affects others, and they gain a voice there.

IFS is not a system where individuals can make decisions in isolation when those decisions affect other people. If a choice impacts others, those people are part of the decision too. At the same time, if a decision only affects you, you are fully free to make that decision on your own without needing approval or input from others.

This creates a natural balance. You are fully free to decide on things that affect only you, but as soon as your decisions have a direct impact on other people’s lives, those who are affected gain a voice in shaping that outcome.

This prevents situations where one person’s freedom becomes another person’s limitation. Decisions are not isolated actions; they are connected to the people they influence.

In IFS, power is not just individual, and it is not blindly collective either. It is contextual. The more a decision affects others, the more shared that decision becomes.

This ensures fairness without removing freedom. You remain in control of your life, while also respecting the lives of others in a structured and transparent way.

## What Are IFS Standards?

IFS Standards are specifications for implementing an IFS system.

They define the schemas, protocols, and rules that describe how the system should work in practice—how power is delegated, how decisions are made, how responsibilities are assigned, and how everything is monitored and verified.

In simple terms, IFS Standards answer the question: “If we want to build a real system based on IFS, how exactly should it behave?”

They provide a clear and consistent blueprint. Any system that follows these standards will operate according to the core principles of IFS, regardless of how it is implemented or what platform it runs on.

An IFS system is not a single application. It is an implementation of these specifications. Different systems can be built in different ways, but as long as they follow the standards, they remain compatible and aligned with IFS.

This makes IFS consistent, interoperable, and verifiable. It ensures that any system claiming to follow IFS actually works the way it is intended to work.
